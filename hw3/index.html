Overview

In this homework, we implemented a path tracer that makes it possible for our rendering to simulate the behavior of light in real life. We first begin by generating rays from a virtual camera and detecting intersections with primitives. Then to handle more complex meshes, I implemented the bounding volume hierarchy by recursively partitioning a scene into axis aligned bounding boxes that can significantly reduce the amount of time it takes to render a scene. After that, we implemented the hemisphere and importance direct lighting methods and noted the differences between those renderings. Then we implemented the path tracing that would allow us to simulate indirect lighting, or light that bounces off multiple surfaces before reaching the camera. Then we finished off by looking to see if rays would converge and, if so, would stop rendering those pixels.
Through this project I was able to connect the knowledge I learned in lecture to the actual code required to render these images. It was also fun to see the difference between renderings through each stage and also when the parameters of number of light rays or number of samples would adjust.

Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection

Walk through the ray generation and primitive intersection parts of the rendering pipeline.
The rendering pipeline is ultimately trying to bridge our 2D pixels to the 3D world. The pipeline begins with generating the ray by first transforming the point from normalized image space to camera space on a virtual space. Once we have the point on the sensor in camera space, we can treat it like a direction vector to transform into world space using the camera-to-world rotation matrix. Thus our final ray has an origin at the camera’s world position with the ray’s near and far clipping planes.
The next step in the pipeline is to sample pixels to reduce aliasing and averaging out the radiance over the given pixel. We update the pixel’s average radiance in the sample buffer to average out the radiance of the pixel. As a side note, est_radiance_global_illumination is returning a debugging color based on either 1. The direction of the ray or 2. The normal of some surface intersected by the camera ray.
Then we need to see what the ray is hitting. In task 3 and 4 we look at the ray’s intersection with triangles and spheres. We are finding the NEAREST intersection point where the t value is within the bounds of min_t and max_t of the input ray where we continuously update max_t as we continue to find a valid intersection such that we only care about objects that are CLOSER than the current closest hit.
