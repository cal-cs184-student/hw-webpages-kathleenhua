Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection

Walk through the ray generation and primitive intersection parts of the rendering pipeline.
The rendering pipeline is ultimately trying to bridge our 2D pixels to the 3D world. The pipeline begins with generating the ray by first transforming the point from normalized image space to camera space on a virtual space. Once we have the point on the sensor in camera space, we can treat it like a direction vector to transform into world space using the camera-to-world rotation matrix. Thus our final ray has an origin at the camera’s world position with the ray’s near and far clipping planes.
The next step in the pipeline is to sample pixels to reduce aliasing and averaging out the radiance over the given pixel. We update the pixel’s average radiance in the sample buffer to average out the radiance of the pixel. As a side note, est_radiance_global_illumination is returning a debugging color based on either 1. The direction of the ray or 2. The normal of some surface intersected by the camera ray.
Then we need to see what the ray is hitting. In task 3 and 4 we look at the ray’s intersection with triangles and spheres. We are finding the NEAREST intersection point where the t value is within the bounds of min_t and max_t of the input ray where we continuously update max_t as we continue to find a valid intersection such that we only care about objects that are CLOSER than the current closest hit. Part 2: Bounding Volume Hierarchy

Walk through your BVH construction algorithm. Explain the heuristic you chose for picking the splitting point.
My BVH construction algorithm is using the Surface Area Heuristic to pick the splitting point. My algorithm begins with iterating through the primitives between the given start and end and computes the bounding box and centroid bbox. The centroid_bbox is expanding to include the current bbox’s centroid. If we found less primitives than our maximum leaf size, we’ll just store the start and end iterators in the node and return the node. Otherwise, we’ll begin with our recursion to find the best axis to split across. To keep the tree balanced, we look for the longest “extent” of the centroid bbox. Then we apply our Surface Area Heuristic or the idea that the probability of a ray hitting a bbox is proportional to its surface area. We can divide the centroids into 12 buckets and then evaluate each boundary as an option to split. Then we can choose the boundary that minimizes the surface area of the left box * number of left buckets plus the surface of the right box * number of right buckets. There’s also an additional check for if all primitives end up on one side of the split then we just split in the median to force the tree to stay balanced.
I chose the Surface Area Heuristic because it improves upon just splitting at the median by estimating the traversal cost of rays so it’s going to produce better acceleration structures than just always splitting in the median.

Part 3: Direct Illumination

Walk through both implementations of the direct lighting function.
In the hemisphere direct lighting estimation, we’re looping through the number of samples to see if a random directioned ray will intersect with an object. If it does, then we’ll get the emission. Then we multiply the light color by the BSDF and the cosine term and divide by the pdf to normalize the sample.
In the light importance, instead of guessing where the light might be, we loop through every known light source in the scene and sample them directly. We do this by looping through the scene->lights and sampling each light. For each light we sample by calling sample_L which gives us the world-space direction toward a point on that specific light, the distance to it, and the pdf for that specific light shape. Then we cast a a ray that’s limited by EPS_F and the distToLight - EPS_F. If the ray hits anything before it reaches the light, the point is in shadow; if it doesn’t, the path is clear and the light reaches the surface. Thus, we can sum up the radiances produced by the sample and divide by pdf.
When the number of light rays differ, not only does the grainy appearance reduce on the object (the bunny in this case), but the shadow is also decreasing in size, which is to be expected. The edges also gain clarity.

Part 4: Global Illumination

Walk through your implementation of the indirect lighting function.
My indirect lighting function starts by calculating the direct lighting at the current hit point. If we’re accumulating the bounces, we’ll add the direct lighting to our total. We also want to check if we’re only looking for one bounce: if we are then we’ll return the one_bounce_radiance. Once these checks are done, we can move on to the recursion. I set the Russian roulette probability to 0.65 to estimate paths of infinite length without actually tracing them. We only recurse if our ray depth is greater than one and if we’re on our very first indirect bounce (where ray depth is equal to our max ray depth), we always continue.
In the recursive call, we sample a new incoming direction using the surface’s BSDF to get the BSDF value, the incoming direction, and the PDF of the sample. We can then construct the next ray starting from the hit point, moving in the direction of w_in. If the next ray hits something, we call at_least_one_bounce_radiance on the next ray which we can store as L_next to be used in our Monte Carlo Estimator. Then we check how light is being passed back up the recursion stack based on isAccumBounces. If we’re accumulating, we add the contribution from the current frame. If the primary ray has reached max_ray_depth, then this is the result of the recursion and we can return our contribution from the frame. Otherwise, we can pass the light back up to the top of the recursion.

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling

Explain adaptive sampling. Walk through your implementation of the adaptive sampling.
Adaptive sampling is a method of implementing Monte Carlo path tracing while reducing the noise. In regular rendering, noise can be reduced by increasing the number of samples per pixel. However, adaptive sampling allows us to instead check for whether pixels converge faster with low sampling rates and stop sampling if it does converge.
My implementation initializes a variable to accumulate the radiance of the adaptive samples, and s1 and s2 to store the running sums and sum of squared illuminance values. We can then trace the samples and generate the camera ray. Then we’ll update the adaptive radiance with our calculated radiance from est_radiance_global_illumination, evaluate the illumination, and then update our s1 and s2 values. Every samplesPerBatch samples, we’ll check for convergence by computing the sample mean, variance and the I value. If the pixel is converged, we want to break. Then we average out the final radiance over the number of samples taken and update our buffer for that pixel.
