Overview

In this homework, we built a cloth simulator using a mass-spring model. We started by constructing the cloth as a grid of point masses connected by structural, shearing, and bending springs. Then we simulated the cloth's motion over time with external forces, Hooke's law spring forces, Verlet integration, and Provot-style constraints to keep the springs from stretching too far in a single step.

We also implemented self-collision handling with spatial hashing so the cloth can fold onto itself instead of passing through itself. One of the most interesting parts of the homework was seeing how much the physical parameters affect the final look. Changing ks, density, and damping made the cloth feel dramatically softer, stiffer, lighter, or heavier, which made the connection between the math and the rendered motion feel very clear.

Part 1: Masses and Springs

We represent the cloth as a grid of point masses stored in row-major order. Depending on the cloth orientation, the point masses are laid out either in the xz plane or in the xy plane with a small random offset in z. We also mark the pinned masses using the pinned indices provided by the scene so selected points stay fixed during simulation.

After building the grid of masses, we connect nearby masses with springs. Structural springs connect left and upper neighbors, shearing springs connect the two diagonal upper neighbors, and bending springs connect masses that are two units away horizontally or vertically. Part 2: Simulation via Numerical Integration

Effects of changing ks

With a very low spring constant, such as ks = 5 N/m, the cloth is much more flexible. It becomes wrinkly very quickly, develops lots of small creases, and sags more as it falls. With a high spring constant, such as ks = 50000 N/m, the cloth resists stretching much more, so the motion looks smoother and the folds are few to none, looking inflexible.

Effects of changing density

At low density, such as 0.5 g/cm^2, the cloth does not move as dramatically and stays relatively flat, with fewer to no strong folds and less shadowing. At high density, such as 500 g/cm^2, the cloth behaves like a much heavier sheet and collapses more forcefully, creating darker and more compressed folds that look more extreme. It also moves for more time as the cloth settles.

Effects of changing damping

With very low damping, close to 0%, the cloth swings aggressively and keeps oscillating for much longer because little motion is lost between steps. With high damping, the cloth falls more slowly and settles more quickly, acting super inflexible.

Part 3: Handling Collisions with Other Objects

For sphere collisions, we check whether a point mass has moved inside the sphere by comparing the distance from the point mass to the sphere center against the sphere radius. If the point mass is inside, we compute the tangent point on the sphere by taking the direction from the sphere origin to the point mass, normalizing it, and scaling it by the radius. That gives us the point on the sphere surface where the mass should lie instead of penetrating the object.

We then compute a correction vector from the point mass's last_position to that tangent point and apply only (1 - friction) of that correction. Using last_position is important because it preserves the sense that the mass moved into the sphere during the last step and is now being pushed back out along a physically plausible path, instead of simply teleporting from its current position. In practice, this makes the cloth slide and drape across the sphere much more naturally.

Plane collisions are handled similarly, but with a signed distance test against the plane. When a point mass crosses to the forbidden side of the plane, we intersect the segment from last_position to the current position with the plane, then offset the corrected position slightly along the plane normal by the cloth thickness so the point mass rests just above the surface rather than repeatedly clipping through it. As with the sphere, friction is used to damp the correction so the cloth can settle instead of jittering.

Part 4: Handling Self-Collisions

We used spatial hashing as the foundation of handling self collisions. We partition the space into boxes where the dimensions are based on the cloth resolution. Each point mass position is then snapped to its containing box and those box coordinates are combined into a hash key. Then we build build_spatial_map, where we loop through the point masses, hash each position, and then insert the point mass into the bucket for that box in the hash table to group nearby masses together.

Then, in self_collide, we hash the current point to get the hash key to find the bucket of other nearby candidates. We iterate through the point masses in the same bucket: if the difference is less than 2 * thickness, we update the point mass's correction vector such that the resulting pair would be 2 * thickness distance apart. Then we sum up all of the correction vectors, average them over the number of collisions, and scale the result by 1 / simulation_steps to get the final correction to the point mass position.

Effects of density and ks

When changing the density, increasing density makes the cloth heavier, so it falls faster and with more momentum, producing stronger folds and a more forceful collapse before settling. Lower density means the cloth is lighter, so it falls downward more gently and tends to form softer, less dramatic folds.

When varying ks, a higher spring constant makes the cloth stiffer, so it resists bending and stretching, causing broader, sharper folds and less sagging. A lower ks makes the cloth more flexible, so it crumples in on itself more easily, creating deeper draping folds and a bumpier overall shape as it falls onto itself. Part 5: Shaders

A shader program is a small program that runs on the GPU as part of the graphics pipeline. Instead of computing every pixel on the CPU, we send geometry and material information to the GPU and let thousands of shader invocations run in parallel. In this homework, the two main programmable stages were the vertex shader and the fragment shader.

The vertex shader runs once per vertex. Its job is to transform vertex positions into clip space for rasterization and to prepare data that later stages need, such as world-space positions, normals, tangents, and UV coordinates. After that, the rasterizer interpolates those outputs across each triangle. The fragment shader then runs on each fragment and uses the interpolated attributes together with uniforms like light position, camera position, and textures to compute the final color. That pairing is what makes lighting, texturing, reflections, and bump effects possible in real time.

Blinn-Phong shading

The Blinn-Phong model adds together three lighting terms: ambient, diffuse, and specular. Ambient is a small constant base illumination so the object is never completely black. Diffuse depends on how aligned the light direction is with the surface normal, so surfaces facing the light appear brighter. Specular uses the half-vector between the light direction and the view direction to create concentrated highlights that make the material look glossy. Together, these three terms produce a simple but convincing approximation of how light behaves on many everyday materials.

Texture mapping

For texture mapping, the fragment shader samples a 2D texture using the interpolated UV coordinates and outputs that sampled color. We used our own custom texture rather than the default one, which wraps a grayscale portrait image across the draped cloth. Because the UVs are interpolated smoothly, the image follows the folds of the fabric and stretches with the geometry rather than appearing as a flat overlay.

Bump and displacement mapping

For both bump and displacement mapping, we used the same non-default tiled fabric-like height texture. Bump mapping only changes the shading normal, so it creates the illusion of small surface detail without changing the actual geometry. Displacement mapping goes further by moving vertices along their normals in the vertex shader, so the silhouette and the geometry itself become visibly altered.

Comparing the two approaches, bump mapping is cheaper and works well for fine detail because it only perturbs normals. The lighting changes make the material look detailed, but the object still has the same true shape. Displacement mapping is more expensive, but it creates a much stronger effect because the mesh itself is pushed outward and inward by the height field. This means the ridges on the displaced sphere feel much more physical, especially around the outer contour.

Mesh coarseness matters much more for displacement than for bump mapping. With a coarse sphere mesh such as -o 16 -a 16, bump mapping still looks reasonably detailed because it only depends on the fragment normals, but displacement has fewer vertices to move and therefore looks chunkier and more faceted. With a finer sphere mesh such as -o 128 -a 128, displacement can represent the height texture much more faithfully and produces smoother, denser geometric variation. In our screenshots, the high-resolution displaced sphere shows more continuous ridges, while the lower-resolution version keeps the same general pattern but with a more polygonal appearance.

Environment-mapped reflections

For the mirror shader, we computed the outgoing view direction from the camera to the fragment, reflected it across the surface normal, and sampled the cubemap in that reflected direction. This makes the material behave like a polished mirror, so the environment becomes warped across the cloth and sphere according to their changing normals.

Extra Credit

Custom shader

For the custom shader, we implemented a procedural animated "Sci-Fi Hologram" effect in Custom.vert and Custom.frag. Instead of trying to make the cloth look physically realistic, this shader was designed to look like an active projected forcefield: bright at the silhouette, partially transparent through the middle, and constantly animated with moving bands of light.

The first main ingredient was a Fresnel-style edge glow. We computed the dot product between the surface normal and the camera view direction, then inverted that value with 1.0 - dot(n, v) and raised it to a power to sharpen the effect. This makes fragments facing the camera stay relatively dim while fragments near the silhouette become much brighter, which is what gives the cloth its hologram-like rim lighting. Because this depends on the current view direction, the glow updates naturally as the camera moves around the mesh.

The second ingredient was procedural animation using time-based sine waves. We passed a u_time uniform from the C++ render loop using glfwGetTime(), and in the fragment shader we combined that time value with the world-space y-position of each fragment inside a sine function. This created tightly packed horizontal scanlines that travel upward across the cloth over time. Since the pattern is generated mathematically, there was no need for a texture map: the animation comes entirely from the shader logic itself.

Finally, we combined these two effects by multiplying the moving scanline signal by the Fresnel glow mask and using that result to drive the emitted green color and transparency. That way, the animated bands show up most strongly near the outer contour of the cloth instead of uniformly everywhere. The final result is much more stylized than the physically-inspired shaders in Part 5 and demonstrates how shaders can also be used for expressive non-photoreal effects, not just realistic lighting models.

Aerodynamic wind and flag simulation

For a second extra credit feature, we implemented a spatially varying aerodynamic wind force to make the cloth behave more like a real flag. In the baseline simulator, external forces such as gravity are applied uniformly to every point mass, which ignores how wind should interact with the cloth's changing orientation. In reality, wind depends on surface area: a patch of cloth facing directly into the flow should catch much more force than a patch aligned nearly parallel to it.

To model this, we applied wind on a per-triangle basis inside simulate by iterating over clothMesh->triangles. For each triangle, we computed the cross product of two triangle edges. That cross product gives an unnormalized face normal whose magnitude is proportional to the triangle's area, so it naturally captures both orientation and exposed surface size. We then took the dot product between that face normal and a dynamic wind velocity vector to determine how strongly the wind was pushing on the triangle.

The aerodynamic force on each face was computed using F_aero = c_drag * dot(n_face, v_wind) * n_face. If the dot product was negative, meaning the wind was striking the back side of the triangle, we flipped the normal before applying the force so the cloth would still respond consistently to the incoming airflow. Once the total face force was computed, we divided it evenly among the triangle's three point masses and accumulated it into their force totals for that simulation step.

To keep the flag from just stretching into one steady shape, we also added spatial variance to the wind field. By sampling sine and cosine waves based on the vertices' x and y coordinates, we generated a fluctuating z-direction component in the wind. This adds small turbulent differences across the cloth surface, which causes the fabric to ripple, shed waves, and flap much more naturally. To showcase the effect, we created a custom flag.json scene that pins the cloth vertically along its left edge so it behaves like a flag attached to a pole.
