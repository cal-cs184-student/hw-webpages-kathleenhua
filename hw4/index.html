Overview

In this homework, we built a cloth simulator using a mass-spring model. We started by constructing the cloth as a grid of point masses connected by structural, shearing, and bending springs. Then we simulated the cloth's motion over time with external forces, Hooke's law spring forces, Verlet integration, and Provot-style constraints to keep the springs from stretching too far in a single step.

We also implemented self-collision handling with spatial hashing so the cloth can fold onto itself instead of passing through itself. One of the most interesting parts of the homework was seeing how much the physical parameters affect the final look. Changing ks, density, and damping made the cloth feel dramatically softer, stiffer, lighter, or heavier, which made the connection between the math and the rendered motion feel very clear.

Part 1: Masses and Springs

We represent the cloth as a grid of point masses stored in row-major order. Depending on the cloth orientation, the point masses are laid out either in the xz plane or in the xy plane with a small random offset in z. We also mark the pinned masses using the pinned indices provided by the scene so selected points stay fixed during simulation.

After building the grid of masses, we connect nearby masses with springs. Structural springs connect left and upper neighbors, shearing springs connect the two diagonal upper neighbors, and bending springs connect masses that are two units away horizontally or vertically. Part 2: Simulation via Numerical Integration

Effects of changing ks

With a very low spring constant, such as ks = 5 N/m, the cloth is much more flexible. It becomes wrinkly very quickly, develops lots of small creases, and sags more as it falls. With a high spring constant, such as ks = 50000 N/m, the cloth resists stretching much more, so the motion looks smoother and the folds are few to none, looking inflexible.

Effects of changing density

At low density, such as 0.5 g/cm^2, the cloth does not move as dramatically and stays relatively flat, with fewer to no strong folds and less shadowing. At high density, such as 500 g/cm^2, the cloth behaves like a much heavier sheet and collapses more forcefully, creating darker and more compressed folds that look more extreme. It also moves for more time as the cloth settles.

Effects of changing damping

With very low damping, close to 0%, the cloth swings aggressively and keeps oscillating for much longer because little motion is lost between steps. With high damping, the cloth falls more slowly and settles more quickly, acting super inflexible.

Part 4: Handling Self-Collisions

We used spatial hashing as the foundation of handling self collisions. We partition the space into boxes where the dimensions are based on the cloth resolution. Each point mass position is then snapped to its containing box and those box coordinates are combined into a hash key. Then we build build_spatial_map, where we loop through the point masses, hash each position, and then insert the point mass into the bucket for that box in the hash table to group nearby masses together.

Then, in self_collide, we hash the current point to get the hash key to find the bucket of other nearby candidates. We iterate through the point masses in the same bucket: if the difference is less than 2 * thickness, we update the point mass's correction vector such that the resulting pair would be 2 * thickness distance apart. Then we sum up all of the correction vectors, average them over the number of collisions, and scale the result by 1 / simulation_steps to get the final correction to the point mass position.

Effects of density and ks

When changing the density, increasing density makes the cloth heavier, so it falls faster and with more momentum, producing stronger folds and a more forceful collapse before settling. Lower density means the cloth is lighter, so it falls downward more gently and tends to form softer, less dramatic folds.

When varying ks, a higher spring constant makes the cloth stiffer, so it resists bending and stretching, causing broader, sharper folds and less sagging. A lower ks makes the cloth more flexible, so it crumples in on itself more easily, creating deeper draping folds and a bumpier overall shape as it falls onto itself.
